Your baby isn't broken. The expectations are.

Helen Ball runs Durham University's Parent–Infant Sleep Lab. Her core argument: most "baby sleep problems" are a mismatch between how human infants evolved to sleep and what modern Western culture expects of them.

The three-mothers thought experiment

The book opens with three mums of three-month-olds: Kristi (US) anxiously planning to wean and sleep-train before her commute resumes; Lizzie (UK) despairing that her baby won't "self-settle" per her tracking app; Yuki (Japan) sleeping alongside her baby with no sense that anything needs fixing. Same biology, three utterly different ideas of "normal." That gap — between biology and culture — is the whole book.

There is no universal standard for baby sleep, nor a "standard baby" to measure yours against.
— Helen Ball. In the UK and US, 25–30% of parents report a baby "sleep problem." In Japan and Korea, it's 5–7% — same babies, different expectations.
